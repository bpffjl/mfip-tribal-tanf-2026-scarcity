# Facilitator Guide: How Scarcity Affects You and Your Clients

**Session:** MFIP/Tribal TANF Conference — Breakout Session 1
**Date:** April 15, 2026, 9:00–10:20 AM
**Duration:** 80 minutes
**Presenter:** Ben Jaques-Leslie

---

## Session Overview

This training introduces frontline MFIP and Tribal TANF workers to the behavioral science of scarcity. The goals are for participants to:

1. Understand how scarcity in various forms changes decision-making and cognition.
2. Know core concepts from behavioral science that explain these effects.
3. Identify opportunities to design systems and interactions that account for scarcity.

The session is structured around content delivery alternating with group discussion. Plan to spend roughly equal time on each. Adjust pacing based on group size and engagement.

**Rough time allocation:**
- Introduction and framing: 10 min
- Scarcity, bandwidth, and tunneling: 15 min
- Group discussion (personal scarcity): 10 min
- Poverty as scarcity / cognitive effects: 10 min
- Behavioral science and hassle factors: 10 min
- Designing for scarcity: 15 min
- Closing reflection and discussion: 10 min

### Slide 5 — What do these people have in common? (Answer)

Reveal the answer:

---

### Slide 6 — Limited Bandwidth

Explain the bandwidth concept:

> "The first thing that happens is that people's bandwidth becomes limited. Bandwidth is the brain's capacity to perform the basic functions that underlie higher-order behavior and decision-making."

Use the road construction analogy:

> "Think of yourself as a road and your thoughts, plans, and activities as cars and trucks. Normally, traffic flows fine. Now imagine you're experiencing scarcity—you're super busy, a deadline is approaching. It's like a lane closing. Traffic slows. Some cars get off at the wrong exit."

Or use the browser tab analogy:

> "It's like having 40 browser tabs open. Each new page loads slower. You're pulling more information through the same bandwidth."

---

### Slide 7 — Tunnel

Introduce tunneling:

> "When people are in scarcity, many begin to tunnel—focusing on the most immediate need and reducing long-term thinking. They don't plan ahead. They just focus on what's directly in front of them."

Share the Minnesota Starvation Experiment:

> "In 1944, researchers at the University of Minnesota dramatically reduced the caloric intake of 36 conscientious objectors to study what happens when people are starved. As the men lost weight, many became obsessed with food. They spent 20 minutes eating their meager rations. When they watched movies, they ignored the dialogue and focused on any food shown on screen. This is an extreme example of tunneling. They had extreme caloric scarcity and tunneled into exactly what they needed: food."

### Slide 8 — Group Discussion: Personal Scarcity

Ask:

> "What are situations in your own life where you experience scarcity?"

Follow-up prompts if needed:
- What did you do to overcome it? What happened to the rest of your life?
- How have you seen participants deal with scarcity?

Allow 10 minutes.

### Slide 9 — Brandi Drew (Quote)

Read or paraphrase the quote, then share the context:

> "I once heard a story on NPR about a woman named Brandi. She worked at a senior living facility in Michigan. One day she was rushing to get home before day care closed, and she accidentally used the company card to buy diapers. Her boss asked her about it. She apologized, but was fired after 10 years on the job. She started working odd jobs. She got a $500 credit card and maxed it out on household supplies, but by the time she paid it off, she owed over $800."

Draw out the two types of scarcity in the story:
- Time scarcity → rushing → a small mistake with big consequences
- Financial scarcity → tunneling on immediate household needs → ignoring the long-term cost of the credit card

> "When someone is in scarcity, they focus on immediate needs. They can't think about medium or longer-term consequences."

---

### Slides 10–11 — Car Trouble ($300 vs. $3,000)

Set up the mall experiment:

> "Researchers went to a mall in New Jersey and stopped shoppers. They asked about income and demographics. Then they posed a question."

Read the $300 version (slide 10). Ask participants:

> "How do you think scores of 'rich' patrons compared to 'poor' patrons on the intelligence test given afterward?"

Pause. Then reveal: no difference.

Transition to slide 11 — the $3,000 version. Ask the same question again.

---

### Slide 12 — 14 IQ Point Drop

Reveal the finding:

> "People with lower self-reported incomes scored 14 IQ points lower on the Raven's Progressive Matrices test after being primed with the $3,000 scenario—but not after the $300 one."

Provide context for the 14-point gap:

> "That's the difference between 'superior' and 'average' intelligence—or between 'average' and 'borderline deficient.' It's a larger deficit than what subjects typically show after staying awake for 24 hours straight."

Emphasize the key point:

> "People with low incomes are not less intelligent. That was clear in the $300 condition, where scores were equal. The $3,000 scenario activated financial stress, which taxed cognitive bandwidth—but only for people for whom that cost was a real concern."

---

### Slide 13 — Poverty Is Chronic Scarcity

Connect back to Brandi's story:

> "Just getting people with low incomes to *think* about financial stress had profound effects on cognition. What are the effects of *living* with real scarcity every day?"

> "People with low incomes must always focus on immediate needs. There's no cushion if they make a mistake. When Brandi focused on diapers, she made a small error with large consequences. When she got the credit card, she solved an immediate problem without being able to weigh the longer-term costs. Those same errors would not have the same consequences for someone with a higher income."

---

### Slide 14 — Behavioral Science

Introduce behavioral science:

> "Where do these lessons come from? A relatively new field called behavioral science, which draws from psychology, marketing, neuroscience, and behavioral economics."

> "The key question behavioral science asks is: Why do people not behave rationally? What causes us to make so many decisions that aren't in our best interest?"

Share two findings as examples:
- Shorter deadlines lead to greater responsiveness than longer ones
- Too many choices leads to people making no choice at all

---

### Slide 15 — Traditional View of Decision-Making

Walk through the linear model:

- Weigh available information
- Assess costs and benefits
- Make the best personal choice
- Take action

> "We can all agree this isn't how we make decisions all the time. Even if we manage it at the grocery store, we don't do it consistently."

---

### Slide 16 — Behavioral View of Decision-Making

Walk through the messier version using the text boxes on the slide:

- *Where'd I put that email?* → information isn't accessible
- *I don't understand what it says?* → confusion about what's being asked
- *When is my paycheck?* → uncertainty about resources
- *Will I ever even need this?* → unclear value
- *Crap! I need to make a decision.* → pressure to decide
- *Uhhh… this one.* → arbitrary or suboptimal choice
- *I'll do it tomorrow.* → procrastination

Share a personal example if helpful. The presenter's original example was navigating private health insurance during a job transition: confusing information, a time limit, no clear instructions.

Ask: "Do others feel like you make decisions a bit like this?"

---

### Slide 17 — Life Gets in the Way

Summarize the behavioral view:

> "Life gets in the way of rational decision-making on the best of days. What's worse is that our social service systems often make these behavioral barriers worse for both workers and participants."

---

### Slide 18 — Hassle Factors

Define hassle factors:

> "Hassle factors cause people to fail to act due to sometimes very small inconveniences. We might intend to mail an important document for weeks, but having to find a stamp and envelope is enough to convince us that tomorrow is a better day."

Give the combined application form example:

> "The combined application form for SNAP and MFIP starts with two pages of dense text. The form is long. Questions are hard to understand. Formatting is inconsistent. Much of the design makes it hard to want to complete—even if you have significant need. Now imagine English isn't your first language."

Prompt about MNbenefits.mn.gov if relevant to the group.

---

### Slide 19 — Group Discussion: Hassle Factors

Ask:

> "In your own work, where do you see hassle factors?"

Facilitator example: IT ticketing systems that are intended to streamline requests but create new hassles—unclear response times, wrong system, no confirmation.

Allow 10 minutes.

---

### Slide 20 — Designing for Scarcity

Introduce the three principles:

> "Our systems don't have to work this way. Three principles from behavioral science can help reshape them:"

1. **Cut costs** — reduce friction and inconvenience (not budget)
2. **Create slack** — build in room for error
3. **Reframe and empower** — treat families as experts in their own lives

---

### Slide 21 — Cutting Costs: Michigan Works!

Describe the case:

> "Michigan Works! in Southwest Michigan targeted UI recipients to help them return to work faster. The problem was low participation. Behavioral researchers worked with the program to diagnose behavioral barriers, then designed encouragement emails: positive tone, personally addressed, concise instructions, plus reminders and planning prompts."

> "UI claimants who received the emails were more likely to set up a session and complete the program. Removing friction—not changing the program itself—improved access and completion."

---

### Slide 22 — Creating Slack: Packing a Car

Use the analogy:

> "Think about packing a car for a camping trip. Someone with a big car can throw everything in without much thought. If they forget something, no problem—there's room. Someone with a small car has to plan carefully. If they forget their tent after the car is packed, everything has to come out and be reorganized."

> "Apply this to money. If your income is sufficient, you don't have to track every purchase, and an unexpected cost is manageable. If you have a low income, you have to do everything perfectly, and any surprise can have cascading consequences."

> "In our programs, we should be looking for opportunities to create more slack—in time, in resources, in process."

---

### Slide 23 — Group Discussion: Costs and Slack

Ask:

> "What costs do our systems put on you as a worker? Are there opportunities to create more slack? What changes could supervisors or the system make?"

Facilitator example: When sending NOITs, being very explicit about exactly what the participant needs to do reduces cognitive load for both the participant and the worker handling follow-up.

Allow 10 minutes.

---

### Slide 24 — Reflection

Prompt participants to write or think individually:

> "How do your own experiences with scarcity affect your work with participants?"

> "You may need all of their paperwork to keep their benefits active, but the system you're working in is complicated and doesn't always make sense. How do the behavioral constraints of your own work affect your interactions with clients?"

If using Mentimeter or a similar tool, flip to it here. Allow 10 minutes.

---

### Slide 25 — Reframe and Empower

Share the story from the Denver conference:

> "A young woman who had grown up in poverty talked about what had inspired her to break the cycle she had seen around her. She said it was a counselor who looked at her and asked what she wanted with her life. She had never been asked that before. She felt listened to. It was a simple act."

Walk through the three strategies:

**Intention language:** Use language that reflects dignity and agency.
- "People with low incomes" rather than "poor people"
- "Participant" or "client" rather than "case" or "recipient"
- "Coach" or "family advocate" rather than "case manager"

**Plausible paths:** Show that change is possible.
- Share peers' successful paths
- Encourage positive norms
- Use concrete action plans
- Example: Students who wrote short reflections after hearing peers describe initial struggles in college increased their GPAs.

**Families as experts:** Make decisions with families, not for them.
- Collect family feedback systematically
- Recognize that families may know why things aren't working (e.g., appointment hours don't match common work schedules)

Note on power dynamics:
> "We need to understand the imbalance of power between ourselves and the people we serve. As one mother put it, staff at benefits offices 'have the power to tell me yes or no.'"

---

### Slide 26 — Group Discussion: Reframing

Ask:

> "How have you tried to reframe your interactions with families to empower them? What would you like to do? What can you do next week?"

Close the discussion with a commitment prompt:

> "Let's each agree to try one thing we've discussed this week."

Allow 10 minutes.

We've talked about how our systems create behavioral barriers for workers and participants alike, and we've looked at some concrete ways people have tried to overcome them."

## Facilitation Tips

- The group discussions are the core of the session. Don't cut them short to cover more content.
- Normalize the experience of scarcity for workers—many participants will have direct personal experience with financial hardship, time pressure, or both.
- Avoid framing participants (clients) as deficient or irrational. The entire point is that their behavior makes sense given their circumstances.
- If the group is large, consider doing discussion in pairs or small groups before opening to the full room.
