# How Scarcity Affects You and Your Clients
### Participant Handout — MFIP/Tribal TANF Conference, April 15, 2026

---

## Key Concepts

**Scarcity** is having less of something than you feel you need. This includes money, time, calories, and social connection. When people experience scarcity, they tend to behave in predictable and similar ways regardless of what type of scarcity they face.

**Bandwidth** is the brain's capacity for planning, decision-making, and self-regulation. Scarcity taxes bandwidth. When bandwidth is depleted, it is harder to think clearly, plan ahead, or avoid mistakes—not because of a lack of intelligence or effort, but because cognitive resources are being consumed elsewhere.

**Tunneling** is what happens when bandwidth is strained: people focus narrowly on the most immediate need and neglect longer-term considerations. This is adaptive in the moment but can create compounding problems over time.

**Poverty is chronic scarcity.** Research shows that simply asking people with lower incomes to think about a large financial problem reduced their scores on a cognitive test by 14 IQ points—an effect larger than going without sleep for 24 hours. Living with real financial scarcity every day has more significant effects. People with low incomes are not less capable; they are operating with less available bandwidth.

**Hassle factors** are small inconveniences that cause people to fail to act, even when they intend to. Complex forms, unclear instructions, and confusing processes all function as hassle factors. They affect both program participants and frontline workers.

## Three Principles for Designing for Scarcity

**1. Cut costs.** Reduce the friction and inconvenience built into systems and interactions. This is not about cutting budgets—it's about reducing the cognitive and logistical burden on workers and participants. Example: Michigan Works! increased program uptake by sending UI recipients concise, personally addressed outreach emails with clear instructions.

**2. Create slack.** Recognize that people with low incomes have no margin for error. A mistake that is trivial for someone with a high income can be catastrophic for someone without financial cushion. Look for opportunities to build in room for error in your processes, timelines, and communications.

**3. Reframe and empower.** Use language and practices that reflect dignity and treat families as experts in their own lives. Three strategies:
- *Intention language:* "People with low incomes" rather than "poor people"; "participant" rather than "recipient"; "family advocate" rather than "case manager."
- *Plausible paths:* Show that change is possible by sharing examples of peers who have succeeded. Use concrete action plans.
- *Families as experts:* Make decisions with families, not for them. Systematically collect and act on family feedback.

## Discussion Questions

### Personal scarcity
What are situations in your own life where you experience scarcity? What happens to the rest of your life when you tunnel on the immediate need?

### Hassle factors
What are examples of hassle factors in your work? Where do they affect you as a worker? Where do they affect participants?

### Costs and slack
What costs do our systems put on you? Are there opportunities to create more slack in your work? What would need to change—and who would need to make that change?

### Reflection
How do your own experiences with scarcity affect your work with participants?

### Reframing
How have you tried to reframe your interactions with families to empower them? What's one thing you could try next week?
